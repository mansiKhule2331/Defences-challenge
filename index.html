Principle 1: The Need to Share Information

The fundamental idea of the Internet is sharing information. Imagine we have information on one computer (Computer A) and we want it to appear on another (Computer B).

The simplest method is carrying a USB drive, but this is slow and doesn’t scale. A better way is to run a cable (Ethernet or Fiber Optic) between them and send signals. A high-voltage signal might represent 1 and a low-voltage 0. This gives us the most basic form of networking: a direct link.

Principle 2: The Problem of Scale

With two computers, direct links are fine. But with three or more, the number of cables grows rapidly. For 100 computers, we’d need 4,950 cables — clearly impractical.

The solution is a central device: a Switch. Every computer connects to the switch, and it forwards messages only to the intended machine. We’ve now invented a Local Area Network (LAN).

Principle 3: The Problem of Connecting Networks

Suppose one office has its own LAN and another office has theirs. How do they communicate? We can’t connect every computer directly. Instead, we connect the networks using a special device: a Router. A router passes data between networks and finds the best path.

Principle 4: Making a Global Network

Now imagine connecting millions of networks worldwide. Routers link together to form a massive web — the Internet. Data is broken into packets, each containing the source, destination, and message.

  • IP Address: Every device has a unique address (like a home address).
  • DNS (Domain Name System): Converts human-friendly names (google.com) into IP addresses.
  • Protocols: TCP/IP ensures reliable communication between devices.

Principle 5: Why Formats and Standards Matter

The Internet works because all computers agree on standards. These include formats for audio, video, web pages, and communication protocols. Without common formats, sharing would be impossible.
